People Management and Legal Policy Landscape

Detailed Description of Relevant Legislation

Access to Information Act

Premised on the notion of transparency, the Access to Information Act gives Canadian citizens, permanent residents and any person or corporation present in Canada a right to access records of government institutions. The Act complements other policies and procedures to make government information publicly available, including open government initiatives and proactive disclosure. It fosters greater accountability and better responsiveness to the needs of citizens.

The Office of the Information Commissioner was established to assist individuals and organizations who believe that their rights under the Act have been violated by a federal institution. In this role, the Office must also ensure that the rights of the government organizations and any third parties involved are respected.

Canada Labour Code (the Core Public Administration is subject only to Part II)

Part II of the Canada Labour Code (the “Code”) sets out the duties of the employer and employees in maintaining occupational health and safety in the workplace. The Code also establishes fundamental employee rights and sets out the roles of health and safety committees and officers as well as procedures for determining whether a danger exists in the workplace. The Code affects both public and private sector workers in industries under federal jurisdiction.

As an employer covered by Part II of the Code, the Treasury Board (and organizations in the Core Public Administration) has a general duty to protect the health and safety at work of every person they employ. Department heads have the authority to issue policy instruments and provide guidance on occupational health and safety issues unique to their organization.

The Treasury Board may use its authority under Part II of the Code to provide direction to departments on issues of occupational health and safety.

Canada School of Public Service Act

Under the Canada School of Public Service Act (CSPSA), the Canada School of Public Service’s (a departmental corporation) key objectives include:

  • Create educational programs for the Public Service;
  • Encourage pride and excellence in the Public Service;
  • Foster a common sense of purpose, values and traditions in the Public Service;
  • Support the growth and development of public servants;
  • Help ensure that public servants have the knowledge, skills and competencies they need to do their jobs effectively;
  • Assist deputy heads in meeting the learning needs of their organization; and
  • Pursue excellence in public management and administration; etc.

Pursuant to the CSPSA, the President, who is also the chief executive officer of the School, is to take into consideration the policies established by the Treasury Board with respect to learning, training and development needs. Further, the Treasury Board is responsible for approving the terms and conditions of employment of the School’s teaching and research staff, as well as the fees for services provided by the School.

Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms

The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms guarantees certain rights and freedoms for all Canadian citizens, including equality rights, minority language educational rights and rights respecting Canada’s official languages.

Canadian Human Rights Act

The Canadian Human Rights Act (CHRA) ensures equality of opportunity and freedom from discrimination based on eleven prohibited grounds, including gender, age, religion, and family status, in workplaces under federal jurisdiction.

The CHRA does not refer to Treasury Board exclusively as the “employer.” Under the CHRA, both federal departments and some private sector employers are required to comply with the CHRA when exercising their respective powers, duties and functions. Prevention of discrimination and duty to accommodate are examples where the application of this Act provides deputy heads with employer functions.

Both the Treasury Board and federal departments share responsibilities under the CHRA for the prevention of sexual harassment and other discriminatory practices. The Treasury Board may set high level strategic direction across the system regarding harassment in the workplace and the duty to accommodate and deputy heads may issue internal procedures, tailored to their operational needs to prevent harassment, protect persons with a disability and resolve discrimination-related issues in their respective organizations.

Employment Equity Act

The Employment Equity Act (EEA) requires federally-regulated employers to engage in proactive employment practices to provide equal opportunities for employment to four designated groups: women; persons with disabilities; Aboriginal peoples; and members of visible minorities. The EEA also establishes the Employment Equity Review Tribunal.

While the Minister of Labour is the minister responsible for the EEA, the Treasury Board and the Public Service Commission are responsible for carrying out the obligations of an employer under the EEA in relation to employees in the federal public administration (as set out in Schedule I or IV of the Financial Administration Act) and works to identify and eliminate employment barriers, institute positive policies and practices, and make reasonable accommodations to ensure a reasonable representation of the employment equity designated groups in the workplace. The President of the Treasury Board is also required to report annually on the state of employment equity in the federal public administration.

Expenditure Restraint Act

The Expenditure Restraint Act (ERA) controls expenditures of the federal government in relation to employment, including setting limits to increases in the rates of pay of unionized and non-unionized employees in the federal public sector for periods that begin between April 1, 2006, and March 31, 2011. The ERA also maintains the right to bargain collectively and established dispute resolution mechanisms (such as a strike by employees or formal arbitration).

The Treasury Board is required to monitor employer compliance with this Act.

Financial Administration Act

The Financial Administration Act (FAA) establishes the Treasury Board as a committee of the Queen’s Privy Council for Canada. It also establishes the President of the Treasury Board, the Secretary, the Comptroller General, and the Public Service Human Resources Management Agency.

The FAA is the legal framework for the general financial management and accountability of public-service organizations and Crown corporations. It sets out a series of fundamental principles under which government spending may be approved, expenditures can be made, revenues obtained, and funds borrowed. 

The FAA is the vehicle under which the Treasury Board (TB) adopts administrative policies for the Government of Canada. It provides TB with the specific authority to issue directives on the management and control of funds. Treasury Board also carries out other related functions, acting as the employer of public service employees engaged in core public administration and playing a key role in real-property matters. Treasury Board may act by approving general or specific policies, directives or standards (e.g. the Policy on Classification clarifies the responsibilities and accountabilities in the area of organizational design and classification). The classification program provides a basis for employee compensation in the core public administration.  It has a direct impact on the wage bill, which is the largest expenditure in the public service.

With respect to the public service, the FAA provides Treasury Board with the authority to act in relation to specific areas of human resources management, including:

  • Determining terms and conditions of employment, pensions, compensation and benefits;
  • Organization of public service and classification;
  • Negotiation of collective agreements with bargaining agents;
  • Establishing policies, programs or directives with respect to strike management, discipline, disclosure of wrongdoing in the public service, prevention of harassment and employment equity;
  • Establishing learning, training and development needs;
  • Awards and recognition;
  • Standards of discipline;
  • Demotion or termination of employees; and
  • Performance management.

The FAA also grants human resource management authorities to deputy heads of separate agencies, subject to any terms and conditions the Governor in Council may direct, and sets out the HR responsibilities of separate agencies, in addition to those of Treasury Board and the deputy heads.

Garnishment, Attachment and Pension Diversion Act

The Garnishment, Attachment and Pension Diversion Act provides for the diversion of pension benefits payable under various statutes, including the Public Service Superannuation Act, to satisfy or partially satisfy an obligation to pay maintenance or support to a spouse, child or other dependant.

Government Employees Compensation Act

The Government Employees Compensation Act provides for compensation, including medical expenses and benefits or allowances, for federal employees (except Canadian Forces or RCMP members) who are injured by an accident arising out of and in the course of their employment or who become ill as a result of the nature of their duties.

National Public Service Week: Serving Canadians Better Act*

The National Public Service Week: Serving Canadians Better Act establishes “National Public Service Week” which is to take place every year in June and includes a series of events that recognize employees of the federal public service.

Official Languages Act*

The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms enshrines language rights and the Official Languages Act (OLA) is considered a quasi-constitutional statute. The OLA establishes the rights of members of the public, official language minority communities and federal public servants.

Under the OLA, the Treasury Board is responsible for the general direction and coordination of federal policies and programs relating to the implementation of Part IV (Communications with and Services to the Public), Part V (Language of Work), and Part VI (Participation of English-speaking and French-speaking Canadians) of the OLA. The Department of Canadian Heritage plays a role in implementing other provisions of the OLA, such as Part VII, which deals with the duty to take positive measures to enhance the vitality of linguistic minority communities and fostering the full recognition and use of both official languages.

The President of Treasury Board must submit an annual report to Parliament on the implementation of programs related to Parts IV, V and VI of the OLA.

Public Sector Equitable Compensation Act

The Public Sector Equitable Compensation Act (PSECA) was enacted in March 2009 and will come into force once regulations have been developed. The PSECA is a human right legislation that affirms that women in the federal public sector should receive equal pay for work of equal value. The Act upholds the government’s commitment to eliminating gender-based discrimination in compensation. This new proactive approach replaces the previous complaints-based regime of pay equity under the Canadian Human Rights Act.

The PSECA applies to federal public sector employers, their employees (unionized and non-unionized), and the bargaining agents. In particular, the legislation applies to the Treasury Board, which is the employer for departments and agencies listed in Schedules I and IV and separate agencies listed in Schedule V of the Financial Administration Act, as well as the Canadian Forces and the Royal Canadian Mounted Police.

The PSECA obliges employers and bargaining agents to proactively ensure employees receive equitable compensation at the time their wages are set. For unionized employees, bargaining agents are jointly accountable with Public sector employers for ensuring that equitable compensation is an integral part of the collective bargaining process. Under the PSECA, employees may file complaints with the Public Service Labour Relations Board.

Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act*

The Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act (PSDPA) creates a regime for the disclosure of wrongdoing in the federal public sector and the investigation of complaints related to reprisals, providing for the protection of public servants who disclose wrongdoing. It also establishes the office of the Public Sector Integrity Commissioner and the Public Servants Disclosure Protection Tribunal.

The PSDPA confers several responsibilities upon the Treasury Board and its president. For instance, Treasury Board must create a code of conduct for the public sector; this code must be reflected in departments’, agencies’, and Crown corporations’ own independently developed codes of values and ethics. The Treasury Board must initiate an independent review of the PSDPA to be carried out five years after its coming into force (i.e. in 2012). The President of Treasury Board is responsible for promoting ethical practices in the public sector and for tabling the annual report prepared by the CHRO to Parliament, informing on activities related to the disclosures made in the public-sector organizations that are subject to the Act. The Treasury Board is also responsible for approving the equivalent disclosure regimes in public-sector organizations that are excluded from the PSDPA (i.e. the Canadian Forces, the Canadian Security Intelligence Service, and the Communications Security Establishment).

Public Servants Inventions Act

The Public Servants Inventions Act (PSIA) establishes the legislative framework for dealing with inventions made by public servants:

  • while acting within the scope of their duties or employment,
  • using facilities, equipment or financial aid provided by or on behalf of Her Majesty, and
  • as a result of or in connection with their duties or employment.

The PSIA provides for the vesting of ownership of such inventions in the Crown, details the responsibilities on the part of the public servant who makes an invention as well as certain responsibilities on the part of the Commissioner of Patents, articulates the responsibilities on the part of the minister of the department which employs the public servant who made the invention (“appropriate minister”) including the responsibility for the administration and control of such inventions, sets out other key rights the appropriate minister enjoys in respect of such inventions, and allows for the appropriate minister to authorize the payment of an award to the public servant who makes such an invention in such amount as the appropriate minister and the public servant may agree on or as the minister determines.

Public Service Employment Act

The Public Service Employment Act (PSEA) sets out the authorities related to appointments and the political activities of public servants. 

It includes a preamble that provides a vision of public service staffing to guide decision-makers.

The PSEA provides that merit and non-partisanship should be the basis of government appointments. It sets out the PSC’s authority to establish policy and oversight, and provides the framework for delegation. It specifies that the deputy heads of organizations have direct authority to establish merit criteria and affirms Treasury Board’s authority over matters such as Qualification Standards and the Public Service Labour Relations and Employment Board’s jurisdiction to hear staffing complaints. 

Other PSEA requirements include statutory priority entitlements, the requirements for area of selection, preference for veterans and Canadian citizens, informal discussion, notification, and the administration of the oath. It includes provisions that enable deputy heads to achieve the objectives of the Employment Equity Act and the Official Languages Act. It also provides for the authority to make deployments, hire casual workers, and lay off employees.

Public Service Labour Relations Act

The Public Service Labour Relations Act (PSLRA) defines the Treasury Board as the employer of public servants in the core public administration (i.e. those portions of the public service listed in schedules I or IV of the Financial Administration Act).

The PSLRA establishes a framework for a collective bargaining regime, which includes the certification of bargaining agents, bargaining rights, unfair labour practices, dispute-resolution procedures including mediation, as well as grievance and adjudication processes. Concerning the adjudication of grievances, the PSLRA establishes the Public Service Labour Relations Board as the adjudicating body. Collective agreements concluded pursuant to provisions in the PSLRA are legally binding on the employer and their representatives, the bargaining agent, and the employees subject to it.

Effective labour-management relations through collaborative efforts between parties is essential for good human resource management. Changes to the PSLRA in 2003 provided for greater cooperation and consultation between the employer and bargaining agents, notably by requiring labour-management consultation committees and enabling co-development to resolve workplace issues.

Public Service Labour Relations and Employment Board Act

The Public Service Labour Relations and Employment Board Act (PSLREBA) establishes the Federal Public Sector Labour Relations and Employment Board (FPSLREB) which merges the Public Service Labour Relations Board (PSLRB) and the Public Service Staffing Tribunal (PSST). The FPSLREB was created on November 3, 2014. Amendments to recourse under the CHRA for federal public servants, as outlined in Bill C-4, will come into effect at a later date. Recourse for public servants will be to the FPSLREB and no longer the Canadian Human Rights Tribunal.

Public Service Superannuation Act

The Public Service Superannuation Act (PSSA) establishes a pension plan for federal government employees. The President of the Treasury Board is the minister responsible for the PSSA, including financial management of the superannuation account and the Public Service Pension Fund. Public Services and Procurement Canada, under the direction of a minister, is responsible for administering the PSSA on a day-to-day basis. The Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions makes periodic actuarial valuations of the Superannuation Account and the Public Service Pension Fund.

Values and Ethics Code for the Public Sector

The Values and Ethics Code for the Public Sector was established by Treasury Board as required by section 5 of the PSDPA and came into force April 2, 2012. It outlines the values and expected behaviours that guide public servants in all activities related to their professional duties. By committing to these values and adhering to the expected behaviours, public servants strengthen the ethical culture of the public sector and contribute to public confidence in all public institutions. Adherence to the Public Sector Code is a term and condition of employment. Public sector chief executives are required, under section 6 of the PSDPA, to establish codes of conduct consistent with the Treasury Board code, applicable to the public servants in their organizations.

Manager’s Guide to Political Activities

You are responsible for providing advice and guidance to your employees regarding non‑partisanship and political activities. Here are the basics of what you need to know:

  • Non‑partisanship is an essential element of both a professional public service and a responsible democratic government. Upholding the non‑partisan character of the public service is the responsibility of all public service employees, including deputy heads and managers whatever their level and duties.
  • The political activities provisions set out in Part 7 of the Public Service Employment Act (PSEA) recognize the right of public service employees to engage in political activities, while maintaining the principle of political impartiality in the public service.
  • The PSEA defines “political activity” as:
    • any activity in support of, within, or in opposition to a political party;
    • any activity in support of, or in opposition to, a candidate before or during an election period; or
    • seeking nomination as, or being, a candidate in a municipal, provincial, territorial or federal election, before or during the election period.

    Note: Employees engaging in political activities must also adhere to the expected behaviours set out in the Values and Ethics Code for the Public Sector and their organization’s code of conduct.

  • Political activities can be divided into two categories:
    1. Candidacy

      Before seeking nomination as, or being, a candidate in a federal, provincial, territorial or municipal election, public service employees must first request and obtain permission from the Public Service Commission (PSC).

      Furthermore, at the federal, provincial and territorial level, public service employees must request, and be granted, a leave of absence without pay from the PSC to be a candidate during the election period. They cease to be an employee on the day they are declared elected at the federal, provincial and territorial level.

      Note: Seeking nomination or being a candidate in elections for Indian bands and school boards does not fall under the political activities provisions of the PSEA but should be considered in light of the Values and Ethics Code for the Public Sector and the code of conduct of employees' organizations.

    2. Non‑candidacy

      Permission from the PSC is not required to undertake political activities in support of or in opposition to a political party or candidate (non‑candidacy). However, public service employees are responsible for examining their specific circumstances to assess and make informed decisions about whether engaging in this type of political activity would impair or could be perceived as impairing their ability to perform their duties in a politically impartial manner. Employees should consider the nature of their current public service duties, the level and visibility of their position, the nature of the political activity and their personal visibility.

  • Anyone may make an allegation of improper political activity against an employee. The PSC may investigate any allegation that an employee may have engaged in an improper political activity. If the investigation establishes that there was an improper political activity, the PSC may take any corrective action deemed appropriate.
  • If you need to provide guidance to an employee regarding political activities, you should seek advice from your organization’s Designated Political Activities Representative (every organization has one!) or visit the PSC’s Web site where you can refer to the Suite of Political Activities Tools. You may also contact the Political Activities and Non‑partisanship Directorate directly at: 1‑866‑707‑7152.

HR Planning and Reporting

Organizations report to the PSC on the staffing that was done during the reporting period. One of the PSC’s roles is to provide oversight of the staffing system. You should be aware that the PSC may ask to see that an organization’s staffing processes is aligned with the HR plan.

Human Resources Planning and Delegated Authority

As you have seen, the deputy head receives his/her staffing authority from the Commission. The Commission expects that staffing will be aligned with the organization’s HR plan. The PSC may request a copy of the organization’s HR plan or, in the course of an audit or a complaint, may seek clarification about the link between a particular course of action and the HR plan.

Keeping Your HR Plan Up‑to‑Date

In our example, the staffing needs of the research facility will change over time. Therefore, it is crucial to keep a HR plan up‑to‑date. This will ensure that you have the right people, in the right place, at the right time.
